# Adversity / Challenge / Failure — master draft

> Master archetype draft (400–600w). Adapt per school to the exact prompt + word limit; don't paste.
> Spend the majority on response and growth, not the hardship itself.

My first six months in Dr. Anand's tumor immunology lab were mostly failure. I ran the same flow cytometry panel three times trying to isolate a specific macrophage subpopulation, and three times the gating fell apart under review — either I couldn't distinguish the population cleanly, or my compensation was off enough that the result wasn't trustworthy. I had told my PI I'd have preliminary data by the end of the semester, and I didn't.

What I did instead was change how I was troubleshooting. I'd been treating each failed run as a full restart — tweak one variable, rerun the whole panel, wait three days for results. I switched to isolating one variable at a time with smaller pilot runs, which felt slower on any given day but let me actually see which step was the problem. It turned out to be antibody titration, something I'd assumed was fine because it had worked in a different assay.

The panel worked on the fourth full attempt. It wasn't just relief — a postdoc who had been stuck on a related problem for almost a year adopted my protocol the following week, and that was the first time research felt like a real conversation instead of a set of instructions I was failing to follow correctly.

What changed for me wasn't the technique, it was my relationship to not knowing. Before that semester I treated a failed result as a personal failure to fix quickly. Afterward I understood it as information, and I got comfortable enough with that to actually slow down and be systematic instead of anxious. That shift matters to me for medicine specifically: a diagnosis that doesn't come easily isn't a personal failure either, and I don't want to be the kind of physician who rushes past that discomfort instead of sitting with it long enough to actually solve the problem.
