## activities

- The work/activities section functions as the "what" companion to the personal statement's "why"; it should let admissions understand exactly what the applicant did.
- A strong activity entry should include four elements:
  - what the experience was
  - what the applicant's responsibilities were
  - what the outcomes or products were
  - what the applicant got out of the experience / why it mattered
- Outcomes should be concrete where possible: publications, initiatives launched, numbers recruited, or other tangible products of the work.
- Within the 700-character limit, clarity and substance matter more than clever structure or literary devices.
- "Show, don't tell" should be operationalized as specific evidence and concrete detail, not decorative writing.

## conflicts / caveats

- This advice is broadly consistent with the existing activities rubric.
- The "four elements" framework is a useful structural check, but not every excellent 700-character entry must allocate equal space to each element; stronger entries may foreground the most distinctive responsibilities or outcomes.
