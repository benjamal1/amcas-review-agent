# Experiences Rubric

**Source note:** The three official AAMC criteria for evaluating meaningful experiences are drawn directly from the 2026 AMCAS Application Workbook (p. 26): *transformative nature of the experience, impact made while engaging in the activity, and personal growth experienced because of your participation.* The five sub-dimensions below are agent-defined based on adcom evaluation conventions — they are not AAMC-sourced.

**What this domain scores:** The *underlying substance* of each experience category — not how it is written (that is Activities) but what actually happened, how much you contributed, and how it shaped you. An exceptional experience with a weak description is scored high here and low in Activities. The goal is to separate signal from presentation.

**Scale:** 1–10 per sub-dimension. Average = experiences_avg.

---

## Sub-dimension 1: Research Depth

Does the research contribution reflect genuine intellectual ownership — hypothesis generation, experimental design, troubleshooting, interpretation — or primarily task execution?

**AAMC anchor:** "Transformative nature" and "personal growth" — did the research change how the applicant thinks, not just what they did?

| Score | Anchor |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | Task execution only. Pipetted samples, entered data, ran established protocols. No ownership of questions or results. |
| 4–5 | Some ownership. Understood the project and contributed reliably, but did not drive design or interpretation. |
| 6–7 | Real contribution. Can explain why the research question matters, made meaningful technical decisions, saw results through to analysis or presentation. |
| 8–9 | Intellectual ownership. Led a sub-project, iterated through failure toward a result, contributed to a publication or poster, has a mentor relationship built on trust. |
| 10 | Exceptional. First/second author contribution, drove design of a novel approach, or produced a result with independent scientific significance. Honors thesis-level engagement. |

Evidence to look for: Hours (sustained ≥ 1 semester), output (publication, poster, thesis), ownership language (lead, designed, iterated), mentor relationship quality, cross-domain breadth.

---

## Sub-dimension 2: Clinical Depth

Does the clinical exposure reflect genuine patient contact, continuity of care, and developing clinical judgment — or primarily observation and task assistance?

**AAMC anchor:** "Impact made" — did the applicant affect patients, not just witness care?

| Score | Anchor |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | Peripheral exposure only. Observed procedures, restocked supplies, answered phones. No direct patient contact. |
| 4–5 | Some patient contact but limited continuity. Greeted patients, escorted, assisted with basic tasks. Patient interaction exists but is transactional. |
| 6–7 | Meaningful patient contact. Conducted intake conversations, assisted with clinical tasks under supervision, developed understanding of care logistics across a range of case types. |
| 8–9 | Substantive clinical role. Progressed in responsibility over time, developed rapport with individual patients, contributed to pre/post-visit care, has seen a range of pathology or patient presentations. |
| 10 | Exceptional. Patient-facing responsibility approaching that of a clinical team member. Trusted with patient communication, care coordination, or procedures under supervision. Clear evidence of clinical judgment developing. |

Evidence to look for: Hours (sustained ≥ 100), setting variety (outpatient + hospital), progression of responsibility, patient relationship language, case variety.

---

## Sub-dimension 3: Shadowing Quality

Does the shadowing reflect active learning — asking questions, understanding decision-making, processing what was observed — or passive observation of procedures?

**AAMC anchor:** "Personal growth" — did exposure change the applicant's understanding of medicine?

| Score | Anchor |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | Passive observation. Watched procedures. No evidence of engagement with the clinical reasoning or relationship dynamics. |
| 4–5 | Some engagement. Recognized interesting cases or moments, but reflection is surface-level ("confirmed my interest in medicine"). |
| 6–7 | Active observation. Asked attending questions, noticed communication patterns, began connecting procedure to patient outcome. Evidence of building a mental model of clinical practice. |
| 8–9 | Mentorship relationship formed. Shadowed one physician longitudinally, was taught directly, understood why specific decisions were made. Can articulate what distinguishes good surgical or clinical practice. |
| 10 | Exceptional. Multi-specialty breadth AND depth in at least one. Clear evidence of clinical curiosity, non-obvious insight from observation, long-term relationship with mentor who influenced professional thinking. |

Evidence to look for: Hours (≥ 50 meaningful > 200 passive), specialty breadth, mentor relationship, specific insights (not just "I saw surgery"), longitudinal vs. one-time.

---

## Sub-dimension 4: Service and Volunteering Depth

Does the volunteer commitment reflect sustained, genuine service — or resume-building? Is there evidence that others were meaningfully served?

**AAMC anchor:** "Impact made" — did participation benefit others, and is that impact specific?

| Score | Anchor |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | One-time or token commitment. Participated in a single event or a few hours over a short period. |
| 4–5 | Repeated but thin. Showed up consistently but role was peripheral and impact on others is unclear. |
| 6–7 | Sustained commitment with meaningful role. Participated over months, interacted directly with people being served, can describe a specific person or moment of impact. |
| 8–9 | Sustained + proactive. Sought out the opportunity independently, took on increasing responsibility, has clear evidence of impact on individuals (not just aggregate statistics). |
| 10 | Exceptional. Long-term, high-touch service where the applicant was load-bearing — not easily replaced. Specific individuals were meaningfully helped. |

Evidence to look for: Hours (sustained ≥ 50 over ≥ 6 months), self-initiative (cold-called, sought out, not required), specific impact language, direct service (not fundraising only).

---

## Sub-dimension 5: Leadership and Teaching Depth

Does the leadership or teaching role reflect genuine responsibility for others' outcomes — or title without accountability?

**AAMC anchor:** "Transformative nature" — leadership transforms when the leader is accountable for others, not just themselves.

| Score | Anchor |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | Title only. Listed as an officer but no evidence of decisions made, outcomes delivered, or people developed. |
| 4–5 | Some responsibility. Organized events, coordinated logistics, contributed to team function — but accountability was shared and outcomes are vague. |
| 6–7 | Real responsibility. Made decisions with consequences, managed team members or students, produced visible outcomes (events, curriculum, publications, improved student performance). |
| 8–9 | High accountability. Led an organization through growth or change, or taught with demonstrable impact on learner understanding. Has a specific person whose outcome they affected. |
| 10 | Exceptional. Built something new, turned around something failing, or produced a learner who can now do what they couldn't before. Impact is specific and verifiable. |

Evidence to look for: Organizational scope (how many people?), new initiatives launched, specific people developed, teaching evidence (TA feedback, student improvement), leadership continuity.

---

## Scoring Process

1. Read `Activities/Activities Master.md` for all entries and hours
2. Group entries by category — Research, Clinical, Shadowing, Service/Volunteering, Leadership/Teaching
3. Score each sub-dimension based on the strongest evidence across all entries in that category (not per-entry)
4. Note which entries drove the score for each sub-dimension
5. Calculate experiences_avg: (research + clinical + shadowing + service + leadership) / 5
6. Update `Agent/scorecard.md` experiences_avg field and recalculate composite
7. Update `Agent/improvement-priorities.md`

**Important:** This domain scores the *substance* of the underlying experience. If an entry has thin description but the hours and context suggest real contribution, score the substance. The Activities rubric penalizes the thin description separately.
